Grokking Beyond Addition: Circuit-Level Analysis of Algebraic Learning in Transformers

Independent research · March 2026

Extends the grokking phenomenon from modular addition to eight algebraic operations — four abelian fields and four non-abelian groups (S₃, D₅, A₄, S₄). Discovers a clean capacity-dependent generalisation boundary: all abelian tasks grok to 100% test accuracy at dmodel=64; all non-abelian tasks memorise but never generalise. Peter–Weyl analysis identifies correct dominant irreps even in non-grokked models, showing circuit formation precedes successful grokking. CKA reveals a shared representational substrate across all eight operations.

What is grokking? Small neural networks trained on structured tasks like modular arithmetic often achieve 100% training accuracy quickly, then sit at near-zero test accuracy for hundreds or thousands of epochs — before suddenly generalising to perfect test accuracy. This delayed generalisation is called grokking.

Prior work (Nanda et al., 2023) explained the circuit behind modular addition. This paper asks: does grokking happen for other algebraic operations? And what determines whether a model can generalise at all?

The answer is surprisingly clean: at dmodel=64, all abelian (commutative) tasks grok; all non-abelian (non-commutative) tasks don't — not because of data size or training time, but because the model simply lacks the embedding dimensions to represent the required algebraic structure.

1-layer transformer d_model = 64 8 algebraic tasks Abelian → grokked ✓ Non-abelian → memorised only ✗ Free T4 GPU · ~3 hrs
1
Abelian / Non-abelian grokking boundary
All four abelian operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, ring addition) grokked to 100% test accuracy. All four non-abelian groups (S₃, D₅, A₄, S₄) memorised perfectly but never generalised — test accuracy stayed below 25%. Same model, same epoch budget, same training accuracy.
100% grok rate (abelian) vs 0% grok rate (non-abelian)
2
Discrete-log hypothesis — geometric evidence
Multiplication mod p reduces to addition in the discrete-log domain. Re-indexing the embedding matrix by dlogg and re-applying the Fourier transform reveals clear dominant peaks absent from the raw spectrum, confirming the model partially learns this re-parameterisation.
2.14× Fourier concentration improvement under dlog re-indexing (9.4% → 20.0%)
3
Circuit formation before grokking
Even in models that never grokked, Peter–Weyl analysis of the embedding matrices reveals energy concentrating in exactly the irreducible representations predicted by group theory. The circuit starts forming during the memorisation phase — the model is not confused about what to learn, just incapable of fitting it at this capacity.
Correct dominant irrep identified in all 4 non-abelian models
4
Shared representational substrate (CKA)
Centred kernel alignment between all 28 pairs of embedding matrices shows uniformly high similarity — including between a fully grokked abelian model and a fully memorised non-abelian model. The most surprising pair: add–S₃ = 0.97. Early-stage embeddings appear driven by positional encoding and loss structure, not task-specific algebraic circuits.
All 28 CKA pairs ≥ 0.80 · mean 0.90 · add–S₃ = 0.97
5
Subtraction is slower than expected
Modular subtraction is algebraically isomorphic to addition (just negate one token). Yet subtraction grokked ~184 epochs later. The conjecture: the model must discover the negation map from data, adding a constant overhead not present in addition. This is a small but seed-consistent and statistically stable deviation from the formal complexity predictions.
Sub grokked at 1767 vs Add at 1583 (same C₁/C₂ complexity score)
6
Ablations rule out dataset size, train fraction, and weight decay
Three controlled ablations confirm the abelian/non-abelian boundary is not an artifact. Subsampling addition to 36 pairs (matching S₃'s dataset) still grokked; S₃ at the same size did not. The relative ordering of grokking delays is stable across train fractions of 50%–90% and weight decay values of 0.1–10.0.
Kendall τ ≥ 0.8 across all ablation conditions
MethodWhat it measures
Fourier embedding analysis Projects the token embedding matrix WE onto a DFT basis. High concentration in a few frequencies = the model uses a clock circuit. Abelian groups allow this; non-abelian ones don't.
Discrete-log re-indexing Re-orders the embedding rows by dlogg(a) then re-applies DFT. A 2× concentration improvement provides geometric evidence that the model encodes multiplication via the isomorphism dlog(a·b) ≡ dlog(a) + dlog(b).
Peter–Weyl decomposition Decomposes embedding energy into irreducible representations of a finite group G using character tables. The dominant irrep reveals which algebraic structure the model is trying to use, even before it generalises.
Linear CKA Centred kernel alignment measures geometric similarity between two embedding matrices, invariant to rotation and scaling. Score of 1 = identical structure; 0 = orthogonal. Used to compare all 28 pairs of trained models.
Logit attribution Decomposes the correct-answer logit into contributions from each attention head, the MLP, and the embedding itself. Identifies which components are doing the algorithmic work.
Grokking epoch CDF Plots the cumulative fraction of seeds that have grokked by epoch t, across 3 seeds per operation. Used to compare grokking timing reliably across tasks.

Grokking
Delayed generalisation in neural networks: training accuracy reaches 100% early, while test accuracy stays near chance for many more epochs before abruptly jumping to near-perfect. First described by Power et al. (2022).
Abelian group
A group where the operation is commutative: a ∘ b = b ∘ a for all elements. Examples: integers under addition, non-zero integers mod prime under multiplication. All irreducible representations are one-dimensional, enabling Fourier clock circuits.
Non-abelian group
A group where order matters: a ∘ b ≠ b ∘ a for some elements. Examples: S₃ (permutations of 3 elements), D₅ (symmetries of a pentagon). Require higher-dimensional irreps to represent, which need larger embedding spaces.
Fourier clock circuit
The mechanism a transformer uses for modular addition: tokens are embedded as sin/cos waves at key frequencies, the attention head computes products of these, and the MLP reads off the sum frequency. Analogous to a clock face tracking angle mod p.
Peter–Weyl theorem
A result from representation theory: any function on a finite group decomposes into irreducible representations (irreps). For abelian groups, irreps are all 1D (just Fourier modes). For non-abelian groups, irreps can be multi-dimensional, requiring more embedding space.
Discrete logarithm (dlog)
For a prime p and primitive root g, dlog_g(a) is the exponent k such that g^k ≡ a mod p. The key isomorphism is dlog(a·b) ≡ dlog(a) + dlog(b) mod (p−1), reducing multiplication to addition in a transformed coordinate system.
Centred Kernel Alignment (CKA)
A similarity metric between two representation matrices, invariant to rotation and isotropic scaling. Measures whether two models are organising their embeddings in a geometrically similar way, even if the specific vectors differ.
Irreducible representation (irrep)
The "atoms" of group representations — the simplest building blocks that cannot be decomposed further. Abelian groups only have 1D irreps (scalar signals), while non-abelian groups have irreps of dimension ≥ 2, requiring higher-dimensional embeddings to encode.
